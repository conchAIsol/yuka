Resurrection Biology: Colossal Biosciences Reveal First Look at Woolly Mammoth, "Yuka"

We are so close to the return of the Woolly Mammoth, as Collosal Biosciences releases their first look of what their first Woolly Mammoth could look like.

In a groundbreaking achievement that feels like science fiction come to life, Colossal Biosciences, the pioneering biotechnology company, is taking the first significant steps toward bringing the woolly mammoth back from extinction. As part of their ambitious plan to resurrect the iconic Ice Age creature, Colossal has recently revealed a stunning first look at what their first woolly mammoth named "Yuka" could look like. This announcement has sparked both excitement and curiosity around the world, raising questions about the future of de-extinction, ecological restoration, and the potential impact of bringing back an extinct species.

Colossal Biosciences, founded in 2021 by geneticist George Church, entrepreneur Ben Lamm, and others, aims to revolutionize genetics by using cutting-edge gene-editing technologies to resurrect extinct species and "re-wild" our planet. Their primary focus has been on the woolly mammoth, an iconic animal that once roamed vast parts of the Northern Hemisphere before disappearing around 4,000 years ago due to a combination of climate change and human hunting.

The company's approach to mammoth resurrection is based on gene editing, specifically CRISPR-Cas9 technology, which allows scientists to manipulate DNA with remarkable precision. Colossal is not aiming to bring back the woolly mammoth in its original form but rather a creature that shares much of the mammoth's genetic makeup while being adapted to today's world. This includes the use of the modern-day Asian elephant as the "host" species, from which key mammoth genes will be inserted to create embryos that exhibit characteristics of the woolly mammoth.

Colossalâs ultimate goal is to create a mammoth-elephant hybrid that will be able to survive in modern Arctic environments. This is part of a broader ecological project to help restore ecosystems that were disrupted when the woolly mammoth and other megafauna disappeared.

The digital renderings of the baby mammoth show a creature with a shaggy coat of fur, oversized ears, and a trunk similar to that of its Asian elephant ancestors, but with distinct features like the short, stocky legs and curved tusks that would be characteristic of a woolly mammoth. The "Yuka" is imagined as a small, cuddly version of its full-grown counterpart, but still with that unmistakable woolly mammoth flairâan adorable, yet surprisingly ancient-looking creature.

These digital depictions give us a glimpse of what we could one day see in real life. However, itâs important to note that while these renderings are based on current genetic research and evolutionary patterns, the actual appearance of the creature might vary as the biological processes unfold.

By reintroducing mammoth-like creatures to the Arctic, Colossal hopes to restore a "Pleistocene Park" effect, where the actions of these animals help manage vegetation and soil, increase biodiversity, and even reduce carbon emissions. The idea is that the mammoths, by grazing and tramping the ground, would convert much of the tundra back into grasslands, which would help sequester carbon and potentially mitigate climate change. The creatures could even help prevent the thawing of permafrost, which releases large amounts of carbon dioxide and methane.

While Colossal's project is ambitious, it's also fraught with challenges. Critics of de-extinction argue that the resources and technology used to bring back extinct species could be better spent on preserving endangered species and protecting existing ecosystems. Others worry about the ethical implications of "playing God" with nature, particularly when it comes to introducing genetically engineered animals into the wild.

Moreover, creating a viable population of woolly mammothsâor mammoth hybridsâraises questions about their long-term survival. Will these creatures be able to thrive in the wild, or will they become a novelty that cannot function as part of the ecosystem they were meant to restore? Additionally, there are concerns about the welfare of the animals themselves, as the genetic manipulation process is complex and carries inherent risks.

âWhat we and the IUCN {International Union for the Conservation of Nature} bring to this is a thoughtful consideration of the factors that led to extinction, and more importantly the functional role of the animal in the ecosystem, such as restoring mammoths to the permafrost or passenger pigeons to the forests of North America.â

Critics of de-extinction point to the need to focus our efforts on saving animals that are alive today but what has emerged out of the work led by Revive and Restore is the potential to use gene editing techniques to save todayâs vulnerable species. If you can modify a band-tailed pigeon to look like a passenger pigeon, why not use the same technology to modify coral to survive temperature changes as the seas continue to warm, or amphibians to survive the fungal disease that is currently decimating their populations in South America?

Colossal has responded to these concerns by stressing the rigorous scientific research and ethical considerations they are employing throughout the process. The company is committed to ensuring that their de-extinction efforts are grounded in sustainability and respect for the natural world.

As Colossal Biosciences moves closer to their goal of creating a living woolly mammoth, the idea of resurrecting extinct species will continue to provoke debate. The question of animal welfare also looms large. Genetically engineered animals, particularly those involved in complicated processes like cloning or gene editing, can experience health issues. Early-stage research into de-extinction has seen instances of deformities, shortened lifespans, and other complications in test subjects. These ethical considerations are part of the reason why Colossalâs team is dedicated to working with experts in animal welfare and ethics to ensure that the creatures they produce can live healthy, fulfilling lives.

In 2003, Jose Folch and his team at the centre for Agro-Nutrition Research and Technology (Spain), became the first people in the world to successfully recover a lost species after they cloned cells from the skin of the last of the bucardos, also known as the Pyrenean ibex. A sub-species of the Spanish ibex, the bucardos suffered from years of hunting before the last of its kind tragically died after a tree fell on it. The DNA of this female animal was inserted into the eggs of domestic goats before being implanted into the wombs of the goats and a few female Spanish ibexes.
